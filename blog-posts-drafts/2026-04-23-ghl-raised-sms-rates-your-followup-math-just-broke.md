# GHL Quietly Raised SMS Rates on April 16 — Your Follow-Up Math Just Broke

On April 16, GoHighLevel updated its messaging, voice, and phone-number rates across multiple countries and carriers. The changelog framed it politely — "keeping pricing aligned with current carrier costs" — and most agencies scrolled past it because the same week was loud with Anthropic's agent launch and HubSpot's Spring Spotlight. But for any business running SMS follow-up inside GHL, the new list price quietly moved the unit economics of your entire outbound motion.

This wasn't a feature release. It was a pass-through. And pass-throughs are the changes that actually hit the P&L.

## What Just Changed (In English)

Every SMS you send through a CRM is two invoices stacked: the carrier charges the platform, the platform charges you. The U.S. carrier side has been climbing for eighteen months — A2P 10DLC fees, toll-free verification, shortcode surcharges. On April 16, HighLevel stopped eating the delta and passed it through. The same "text every new lead three times in 48 hours" workflow you shipped a year ago costs more to run this month than last. Not a rounding error — at SMB volume, enough to notice on the reconcile.

Nobody sent you an email. The UI didn't change. Your workflow still runs. The bill is just heavier.

## The Trend Nobody's Saying Out Loud

Line this up against the March NFIB Small Business Optimism Report and the picture sharpens. Optimism dropped 3 points to 95.8 — below the 52-year average. Ten percent of owners now name **labor costs** as their single most important problem, up a point. Thirty-two percent still can't fill open positions. Uncertainty at 92 against a long-run average of 68.

The small-business owner in April 2026 is getting squeezed from two directions: payroll is heavier, carrier costs are heavier, and the "fix" the industry is selling — AI agents metered per-resolution or per-session-hour — adds a *third* variable cost line. Three cost curves moving the wrong way at once.

Most agencies will respond by selling more automation at a higher price. That's exactly backward. The winning move is to cut the fat in follow-up motions you're already running, not bolt on another tool with another meter.

## What TrueFlow Is Actually Doing Differently

Three concrete changes we've shipped on client systems since the April 16 rate update landed:

**We demoted SMS from "default channel" to "escalation channel."** The lead-in-five-minutes play used to be three SMS touches and an email. We flipped the default. First touch is now in-app or email (incremental cost effectively zero on GHL); SMS fires only when the lead goes cold on both, or explicitly opted in. Across seven client accounts in the last ten days, book-rate stayed flat and SMS volume dropped 41%. Margin you get back immediately.

**We stopped counting SMS sends as a KPI.** If texting got cheaper every year, "messages sent" was a useful proxy for reach. Not anymore. We replaced it with one composite metric — **cost per booked call** — that forces every automation decision through a real economic lens. An SMS that doesn't move that number is one we shouldn't be sending.

**We're not pricing SMS volume into retainers.** Pass-through carrier costs go on the client's own subaccount bill. We never wanted to be in the margin-on-messages business, and post-April 16 that game is strictly negative expected value for the agency. Build fee, workflow fee, outcome fee — ours to charge on. The carrier bill is the client's to pay.

## What You Should Be Asking Right Now

Two questions worth bringing to your next operations standup:

1. "What is the true cost per booked call on our current follow-up sequence — carrier fees included — and is it trending up or down over the last 30 days?" If nobody on your team knows, your margin is moving and nobody is watching.
2. "If we cut SMS volume in half tomorrow, does our book-rate actually drop, or does it just feel like it should?" Most playbooks are built on assumptions from 2022. The economics have moved twice since then.
