On April 1, ADP's National Employment Report landed with a number the financial press mostly skipped: small businesses with **fewer than 20 employees added 112,000 jobs in March 2026**. Medium and large employers, same month, went **net negative**. Q1 total: tiny shops added roughly **169,000 people** — out-hiring every larger employer-size category combined.

ADP buried the harder numbers below the fold. **Turnover at firms with fewer than 50 employees hit 3.9%** — the lowest in nine years of ADP tracking. **32% of small business owners** report unfilled openings (historical average: 24%). **45% of those actively hiring** say few or no qualified applicants are even applying.

If you own a small business in 2026, you are doing all the net hiring in this economy and disproportionately staring at an empty applicant pool. The April ADP print drops Tuesday, May 6. The shape is unlikely to change.

## What just changed (in plain English)

For a decade, "labor shortage" was a Wall Street Journal headline. In Q1 2026, it's your inbox. Big companies are running headcount-down strategies because they can — automation budgets, scale, offshore arms. Small businesses don't have those. So when an ops coordinator quits, the next 90 days play out the same in every shop: post the role, get six unqualified applicants, raise the price, re-post, watch the existing team absorb the queue.

The seat is also harder to refill from inside. Record-low turnover at this level means the pipeline is empty *and* the chairs are full. No room for growth without burning out the team already in the building.

Most agencies are still selling "more leads." Wrong sale for May 2026. The bottleneck isn't demand. It's the seat behind the keyboard that handles the lead the moment it lands.
